Chaldean stargazing and astrology have their roots deeply entrenched in ancient Mesopotamia, specifically within the Chaldean culture that thrived in present-day Iraq around the 9th century BCE. As masters of the esoteric arts, the Chaldeans played a crucial role in shaping the history of astrology and its practice throughout the ages.

In its inception, Chaldean stargazing highly focused on observational astronomy, with an emphasis on tracking the movement of celestial bodies such as stars, planets, the Moon, and the Sun. Their approach combined empirical, practical, and religious elements, with the objective of discerning the messages of the gods and understanding the influence of celestial events on human affairs and natural phenomena. Chaldean priests would meticulously record astronomical data and interpret them, laying the foundation for the astrological practices that followed.

Several key figures contributed to the development of Chaldean astrology. One such individual was the Babylonian priest Berossus, who established a school of astrology on the Greek island of Kos in the 3rd century BCE. Berossus's teachings played a significant role in disseminating Chaldean astrological knowledge to the Hellenistic world, ultimately influencing the evolution of Western astrology.

Throughout history, socio-political events and religious beliefs have had substantial impacts on the development and interpretation of Chaldean astrological practices. The Achaemenid Persian Empire's conquest of Babylon in the 6th century BCE, followed by the region's absorption into the Hellenistic Seleucid Empire and then the Arab-Islamic era, all contributed to the exchange, adaptation, and synthesis of astrological knowledge across cultures.

Furthermore, Chaldean astrology was deeply interwoven with religious beliefs, as it was believed that the celestial bodies were manifestations of the gods, which helped transmit divine messages. This led to the development of a complex system of planetary exaltations, wherein each planet was assigned to a specific sign of the zodiac, believed to enhance their powers and divine influence.

Zodiacal signs, which later became an essential aspect of Western astrology, can be traced back to the Chaldeans. They divided the ecliptic into 12 sections, each associated with a constellation. By doing so, the Chaldeans laid down the foundational principles of the zodiac system that astrologers still use today.
