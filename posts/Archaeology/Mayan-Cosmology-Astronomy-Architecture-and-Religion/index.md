The ancient civilization that effectively utilized both astronomical knowledge and well-documented spiritual practices within their cultural heritage was the Mayan civilization. The Maya, located in modern-day Mexico and Central America, were prominent between 250 CE and 900 CE in what is known as the Classic Period. Their impressive architectural achievements and intricate belief systems were symbiotically interconnected, providing profound insights into their understanding of the cosmos and the potential role it played in steering the course of their society.

The Mayans had an advanced system of astronomy, which they integrated into their religion, daily life, and architecture. Their keen observation of celestial bodies allowed them to develop a complex and sophisticated calendar system, which included the Haab, a solar calendar, and the Tzolk'in, a sacred calendar. The two systems exhibited an interlocking manner that would make up the Calendar Round – a cycle that repeats every 52 Haab years.

Mayan astronomical expertise is prominently displayed in their architecture. Their temples and buildings were often designed and aligned following celestial patterns. For instance, the famous El Castillo pyramid at Chichen Itza features a phenomenon known as the "Descent of Kukulkan" during equinoxes. When the sun sets on these days, the corner of the pyramid casts triangular shadows on the northern stairway, creating a visual illusion of a serpent descending the steps. Kukulkan, the feathered serpent deity, was believed to be associated with agriculture and fertility. This astronomical phenomenon reflects the Mayan's appreciation for the connection between the cosmos, time, and the natural world.

The Mayans' spiritual belief system integrated their knowledge of astronomy to a great extent. Mayan cosmology contained multiple layers of heavens and underworlds, with the Earth lying in between. They believed that life was cyclical – a notion that resonated in their astronomical observations, like the movement of the sun, moon, and planets. Various celestial events, such as solstices, equinoxes, and eclipses, held religious significance and were accompanied by rituals and ceremonies.

The relationship between architecture, spiritual practices, and astronomical knowledge manifested in various ways in Mayan society. For example, the observatory at Palenque, known as the "Observatory Tower," is a spiral-like structure built to monitor Venus's movement. Venus played a crucial role in Mayan mythology and was associated with war and agriculture. Its movements were believed to act as a harbinger for events such as conflicts and planting seasons. 

Another impressive depiction of the integration between astronomy and their belief system can be seen in the Dresden Codex. This pre-Columbian Maya book comprises detailed astronomical tables, which included eclipse predictions and Venus's synodic cycle. The codex also contains information on rituals, ceremonies, and divination practices. This amalgamation of scientific knowledge and religious practices demonstrates the convergence of the two elements in Mayan society.

In conclusion, the Mayan civilization serves as a prime example of an ancient society that interwove their knowledge of astronomy with spiritual practices within their cultural heritage. Their remarkable architectural achievements and belief systems provide insights into their understanding of the cosmos and the role it held in their society. The way the ancient Maya incorporated astronomy in their religion, daily lives, and architecture demonstrates their belief in the cyclical nature of life and the importance of celestial events in determining the course of their society. The temples, codices, and celestial alignments reflect a deep connection between the physical and spiritual realms, illustrating the comprehensive and intertwined nature of the Mayan's multidimensional world view.
