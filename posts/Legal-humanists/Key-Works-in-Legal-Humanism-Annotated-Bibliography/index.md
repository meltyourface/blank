## I. Introduction

The Legal humanist movement originated in the 12th century and surged in importance during the Renaissance period. Legal humanists sought to re-examine the foundations of law by engaging with ancient Roman and Greek texts. This annotated bibliography serves as an essential resource for understanding the key works and contributors to the field of Legal humanism.

### II. Legal humanist works

1. Accursius (c. 1182-1260) - Glossa Ordinaria (Standard Glosses)
    - Contains legal commentary on Roman law, offering interpretations of the Corpus Juris Civilis
    - Accursius' work was widely adopted by European law schools, significantly shaping legal scholarship

2. Francisco de Vitoria (1483-1546) - Relectiones Theologicae (Theological Reflections)
    - Analyzes the concept of natural law and just war theory
    - Asserts that international law should apply universally to all peoples, irrespective of their religion or culture

3. Andrea Alciato (1492-1550) - Emblematum Liber (Book of Emblems)
    - Bridges the gap between ancient Roman legal thought and Renaissance humanism
    - Addresses legal ethics and jurisprudence, employing allegorical emblems to illustrate key concepts

    - Examines the legal and economic implications of Roman coinage
    - Provides a comparative evaluation of Roman and contemporary currency systems

5. Hugo Grotius (1583-1645) - De Jure Belli ac Pacis (On the Law of War and Peace)
    - Delves into the principles of international law, natural law, and human rights
    - Marks one of the earliest instances of systematic legal writing on warfare
    - Significantly impacted the future development of international law

6. Bartolus de Saxoferrato (1313-1357) - Commentaria in Quatuor Libros Sententiarum (Commentaries on the Four Books of Sentences)
    - Explores legal concepts such as property rights, contracts, and torts
    - Presents jurisprudential insight, demonstrating the importance of analyzing legislation in both historical and contemporary contexts

7. Cino da Pistoia (1270-1336) - Lectura in Codice (Lectures on the Codex)
    - Offers critical analysis of Roman legal concepts, grounding them in humanist ideals
    - Influenced generations of legal scholars, including Bartolus de Saxoferrato

### III. Comparative evaluations

- Accursius vs. Bartolus de Saxoferrato: While both contributed significantly to the study of Roman law, Accursius' work primarily focused on textual interpretation, whereas Bartolus engaged more directly with the practical application of the law, thus furthering the legal humanist tradition.
  
- Hugo Grotius vs. Francisco de Vitoria: Both scholars made foundational contributions to the development of international law. However, Grotius provided a more systematic and comprehensive account of warfare and peace, whereas Vitoria focused more on the ethical aspects of natural law and just war theory.

### IV. Conclusion

This annotated bibliography offers a comprehensive overview of seminal Legal humanist works and highlights notable contributions that have significantly impacted the domain's intellectual landscape. By examining key texts and comparing their approaches to various legal concepts, we can better understand the rich history and development of Legal humanism.
