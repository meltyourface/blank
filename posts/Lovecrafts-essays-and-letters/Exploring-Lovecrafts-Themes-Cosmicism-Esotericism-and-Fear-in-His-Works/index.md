In order to answer this knowledge challenge, let us first decipher the three main thematic concepts of Lovecraft's essays and letters which are cosmicism, esoteric knowledge, and the fear of the unknown. With a clear understanding of these themes, we can then delve into their interrelationship and how they are manifested in Lovecraft's works, thereby reflecting his philosophical outlook on humanity's place in the universe.

Cosmicism, at its core, is a philosophical framework that argues that human beings are insignificant in the grand design of the universe. It postulates that intelligent beings exist beyond the human comprehension, and it emphasizes the vast scale of the cosmos and our inability to perceive or recognize it. Cosmicism also suggests that we cannot fathom the motives or designs of these entities, and that their existence is beyond our control or influence.

Esoteric knowledge, on the other hand, is the idea that there are hidden or secret forms of knowledge accessible only to a select few who are initiated or in search of wisdom. Lovecraft weaves esoteric knowledge into his essays and letters by introducing his readers to unattainable and arcane information. He implies that curiosity and a thirst for the truth might lead one to uncover unsettling knowledge, often with dire consequences.

The fear of the unknown is another recurring theme present in Lovecraft's works. While he often showcases this fear in his fictional stories, it is equally palpable in his essays and letters. Lovecraft explores the way fear can manifest itself when humans are confronted with something alien and inexplicable and demonstrates that this fear is a natural and primal response.

These themes are all intricately interconnected in Lovecraft's philosophical outlook on humanity's place in the universe. In Lovecraft's view, the universe is an immense, uncaring, and unfathomable expanse, and humans are mere specks within it. The cosmic perspective highlights the insignificance of humanity, and the pursuit of esoteric knowledge only serves to reinforce this notion as it reveals the unsettling truths underlying existence. The fear of the unknown acts as a cautionary element, warning humanity against probing too deep into the secrets that lay hidden.

There are several works by Lovecraft which encapsulate these themes, with "The Call of Cthulhu," "At the Mountains of Madness," and "The Shadow Out of Time" being some of the best examples. In "The Call of Cthulhu," Lovecraft weaves the themes of cosmicism, esoteric knowledge, and the fear of the unknown to tell a story of discovering an ancient, alien civilization and unveiling the existence of a malevolent cosmic entity. "At the Mountains of Madness" delves into the exploration of an unfathomable Antarctic city, emphasizing the sheer insignificance of humanity and our place in the cosmic order. Finally, "The Shadow Out of Time" explores the idea of transcending time and space, and the notion of eternal cosmic entities controlling and shaping the history of mankind.

In summary, Lovecraft's essays and letters contain overarching themes of cosmicism, esoteric knowledge, and the fear of the unknown, which, when intertwined, contribute to his philosophical outlook on humanity's place in the universe. These themes are especially exemplified in works such as "The Call of Cthulhu," "At the Mountains of Madness," and "The Shadow Out of Time," which highlight humanity's fragility, our insignificance in the vast cosmic scheme, and the hazards of dabbling in knowledge beyond our understanding.
