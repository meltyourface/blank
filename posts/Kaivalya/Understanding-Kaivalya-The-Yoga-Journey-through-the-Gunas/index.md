To begin responding to this knowledge challenge, we must first establish a common understanding of the key terms involved in this question. 

Kaivalya is a Sanskrit term that refers to a state of complete liberation or detachment from the material world and the realization of one's true nature. It is the ultimate goal of the spiritual journey in several Indian philosophical traditions, including Yoga.

Yoga, in this context, refers to the complete system of physical, mental, and spiritual practices designed to attain self-realization, liberation, and unity with the universal consciousness.

The three primary modalities of Nature, also known as the Gunas, consist of Sattva (balance, purity, knowledge), Rajas (activity, passion, motion), and Tamas (inertia, darkness, ignorance).

With these definitions established, we can now begin to address the primary challenge: how the process of attaining Kaivalya through Yoga intersects with the recognition of the Gunas to transform an initiate's understanding of self, leading to the dissolution of ego-bound identification and the experience of non-dual consciousness.

The process of attaining Kaivalya through Yoga involves the practice of eight core components, also known as Ashtanga Yoga or the Eight Limbs of Yoga, including:

1. Yama (ethical restraints)
2. Niyama (personal observances)
3. Asana (physical postures)
4. Pranayama (breath control)
5. Pratyahara (withdrawal of the senses)
6. Dharana (focused concentration)
7. Dhyana (meditation)
8. Samadhi (ultimate absorption in the non-dual consciousness)

In the course of the Yoga journey, an initiate comes to recognize and understand their primary modality of Nature and its impact on their personality, actions, and thoughts. By engaging in the practice of Yoga, the individual aims to balance and transcend the influence of the Gunas, moving from a state dominated by Tamas and Rajas to one that is characterized by Sattva.

As practitioners deepen their Yoga practice, they are likely to experience increased levels of self-awareness and introspection. Through consistent practice, they come to understand that their ego-bound identification is a result of their limited and conditioned perception of self, which is influenced by the Gunas.

This realization becomes the starting point for the dissolution of the ego-bound identification. As individuals actively strive to balance and refine the Gunas through their Yoga practice, they simultaneously weaken the ego's grip on their consciousness. Once the ego has been dissolved, a practitioner can fully experience non-dual consciousness, which is the ultimate state of existence, where subject and object merge into a unified whole, also known as Kaivalya.
