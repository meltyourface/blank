For this comprehensive exploration, I have chosen to delve into the world of Alchemy.

Alchemy, the ancient art of transmuting base elements into more precious ones, contains rich and complex historical roots. Its origins are usually traced back to Hellenistic Egypt, but its influence has spanned centuries and numerous civilizations. In this annotated timeline, I detail the critical moments of Alchemy's history, from its foundations and evolution to its modern resurgence.

1. 3rd century CE - Alchemy's origins can be traced back to Hellenistic Egypt, where metallurgy and mysticism combined, sparking a tradition of spiritual transformation and tapping into the demiurge of creation.
 
2. 6th-7th centuries CE - Influential early Islamic alchemists such as Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber) blend practical chemistry and spiritual philosophy while pursuing the Philosopher's Stone, said to hold the power to transform base metals into gold and grant immortality.

3. 13th century CE - Alchemy gains prominence in Europe, thanks in part to the translations of Persian and Arabic alchemical texts into Latin. Medieval alchemists such as Albertus Magnus and Roger Bacon incorporate Christian imagery and ideology into their work.

4. Mid-15th century CE - The emergence of Rosicrucianism sees radical new interpretations of alchemical lore. This secret society, which intertwines alchemical symbolism and techniques with Christian mysticism and Hermeticism, plays a pivotal role in alchemical tradition.

5. 16th-17th centuries CE - Alchemy's "golden age," including figures like Paracelsus, Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa, and Sir Isaac Newton. During this time, alchemists incorporate previously unexplored occult disciplines like Kabbalah and Gnosticism into their practices.

7. 19th century CE - The occult revival spearheads a resurgence of interest in esoteric disciplines. Alchemy gains new adherents, and the formation of organizations like the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn and the Theosophical Society incorporate alchemical principles into their teachings.

8. 20th-21st centuries CE - Renowned psychoanalyst Carl Gustav Jung frames alchemy as a symbolic representation of the process of individuation, a key element in his theories of depth psychology. In contemporary culture, alchemy continues to be explored and reinterpret, capturing the imagination of many spiritual seekers, artists, writers, and researchers.

In conclusion, alchemy's long and vibrant history highlights its continual process of evolution and adaptation. From its origins in Hellenistic Egypt, through the syncretic interaction of various mystical traditions, alchemy has always managed to influence and adapt to changing landscapes. As we move into the future, alchemy's symbolic allure and enduring spiritual relevance will likely continue to influence esoteric explorations and shape human understanding of the mysterious and ineffable.
