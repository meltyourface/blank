To fully analyze the fable of the Tortoise and the Hare and connect its symbolic representation and allegorical meaning with the principles of the Hermetic axiom, "As above, so below," and the Three Initiates' Kybalion, we must first understand the fable, the Hermetic axiom, and the teachings of the Kybalion.

The Fable of the Tortoise and the Hare:

The fable of the Tortoise and the Hare, attributed to the ancient Greek storyteller Aesop, tells the story of a race between a fast, boastful hare and a slow, persistent tortoise. In the story, the hare is so confident in his speed that he stops to nap midway through the race and awakens to find that the tortoise has won. The moral of the story is commonly interpreted as "slow and steady wins the race" or that persistence and dedication overcome natural talent.

The Hermetic Axiom - "As Above, So Below":

The Hermetic axiom, "As above, so below," is derived from the ancient Emerald Tablet of Hermes Trismegistus, reflecting that the microcosm (individual human experience) mirrors the macrocosm (the universe or cosmic experience). This axiom suggests that our mind and consciousness have the power to influence and manipulate our reality through thought and intention.

The Kybalion:

The Kybalion is a foundational text on Hermetic philosophy, attributed to the mysterious group known as the Three Initiates. The book contains a set of seven principles, which are considered fundamental to understanding and applying the Hermetic philosophy in daily life. Among these principles are the concepts of mentalism, correspondence, and vibration.

Connecting the Fable with the Hermetic Axiom and the Kybalion:

1. **Persistence**: Within the fable, the tortoise's persistence and dedication lead to its victory, while the hare's arrogance and lack of focus result in its defeat. This lesson is echoed in the Hermetic axiom and the Kybalion through the emphasis on the power of intention and knowledge about the workings of the universe. In both teachings, the importance of persistence and consistent effort in manifesting desires and shaping reality is paramount. 

2. **The Nature of Reality**: The tortoise and the hare's race can be seen as an allegory for the nature of reality in the Hermetic axiom and the Kybalion. The Hermetic axiom posits a mirrored relationship between the microcosm (the tortoise and hare) and the macrocosm (the universe). The Kybalion furthers this idea, suggesting that all of reality is governed by certain immutable principles. Thus, the fable demonstrates the application of these principles in a more tangible, relatable example, reminding us that the patterns we see in nature can be applied to our own lives.

In conclusion, the fable of the Tortoise and the Hare teaches valuable lessons about persistence, the nature of reality, and the manifestation of desires, which find resonances in the Hermetic axiom, "As above, so below," and the Three Initiates' Kybalion.
