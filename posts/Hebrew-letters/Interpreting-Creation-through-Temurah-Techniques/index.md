Let's take the word "Bereishit" (בראשית) as our starting point. This is the first word of the Torah and can be translated as "In the beginning" or "In the beginning of" (as it refers to the creation of the world).

Now, we'll apply the four mentioned Temurah techniques and interpret the hidden meaning of the words obtained:

1. **Atbash**:
בראשית -> שיתמק
שיתמק (Shitmok) doesn't have an apparent meaning that could be derived from it. However, "Shit" could mean "foundation," representing the foundation of the world and time. "Mok" might mean "compressed". So, it could be interpreted as the creation being a condensed or compressed foundation of existence.

2. **Albam**:
בראשית -> לצחסל
לצחסל (Latzesal) doesn't have a clear meaning either. However, Latz has a connotation of secret or hidden. Thus, it could imply that the process of creation was a secret and inscrutable event.

3. **Achbi**:
בראשית-> דגשכג
דגשכג (Dagshakag) doesn't form coherent words when analyzed directly. **However, in Kabbalistic interpretations, one can attempt to find single-letter significance as well. For instance**:
- ד (Dalet) represents "daat" or "knowledge"
- ג (Gimel) symbolizes "gimel" or "giving"
- ש (Shin) represents spiritual energy

In this context, Dagshakag could signify the divine knowledge of creation and the act of giving spiritual energy.

4. **Ayak Bakar**:
בראשית -> כחעתכע
כחעתכע (Kachatakua) doesn't create recognizable words either. However, Kach could mean "power" or "strength," and Takuah can mean "observed" or "discovered." Consequently, this combination could say that the divine power is observed in the creation process.

In summary, the application of these Temurah techniques to "Bereishit" doesn't provide a coherent narrative or straightforward interpretation, though it does evoke themes of foundation, secret acts, divine knowledge, and power inherent in the act of creation. Such esoteric interpretations can often reveal new dimensions about the source material, allowing for deeper contemplation and understanding of the sacred texts.
