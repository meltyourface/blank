Concept: The Tree of Life

Ancient Inception:

The concept of the Tree of Life can be traced back to the ancient Sumerian civilization, where it was represented as a sacred tree and had associations with fertility, immortality, and knowledge. The Sumerians believed that the tree connected the underworld, the world of humans, and the heavens. This early interpretation laid the foundation for its continued presence in esoteric and occult thought.

Cultural Adaptations:

Throughout history, the Tree of Life has been adapted and interpreted by numerous cultures, including the Babylonians, Egyptians, Norse, and Jewish civilizations. Each culture incorporated their own unique symbolism, mythology, and religious beliefs into the concept. 

In Kabbalistic tradition, the Tree of Life became a central symbol to represent the ten divine emanations known as Sephiroth, through which God manifests and interacts with the world. The Tree of Life, in this context, serves as a map of the universe and a path to spiritual enlightenment for the practitioner.

Significant Occult Figures, Organizations, and Texts:

1. Isaac Luria (1534-1572) was a significant Kabbalist who developed a new interpretation of the Tree of Life, known as Lurianic Kabbalah. He posited that the Sephiroth were further divided into Partzufim (divine personas) and that the fractured structure of the Tree was the result of an event known as the Tzimtzum, in which God contracted itself to create the universe.

2. The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, a secret society active during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, adopted the Tree of Life as a central symbol of their esoteric teachings. Founders Samuel Liddell MacGregor Mathers and William Wynn Westcott used the Kabbalistic Tree to create what would become known as the Golden Dawn system of ritual magic. 

3. Aleister Crowley, a prolific occultist and founder of the Thelemic religion, reinterpreted and expanded upon the Tree of Life and its symbolism in his major text "Liber 777". Crowley emphasized the importance of the practitioner's individual connection and experience with the various Sephiroth and pathways, rather than a strict adherence to previously established systems.
