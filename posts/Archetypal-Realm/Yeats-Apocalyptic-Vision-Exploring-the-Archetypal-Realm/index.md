For this analysis, I have chosen the poem "The Second Coming" by W.B. Yeats, a work that has often been interpreted through the framework of the Archetypal Realm. **The poem reads as follows**:

Turning and turning in the widening gyre
The falcon cannot hear the falconer;
Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold;
Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world,
The blood-dimmed tide is loosed, and everywhere
The ceremony of innocence is drowned;
The best lack all conviction, while the worst
Are full of passionate intensity.

Surely some revelation is at hand;
Surely the Second Coming is at hand.
The Second Coming! Hardly are those words out
When a vast image out of Spiritus Mundi
Troubles my sight: somewhere in sands of the desert
A shape with lion body and the head of a man,
A gaze blank and pitiless as the sun,
Is moving its slow thighs, while all about it
Reel shadows of the indignant desert birds.

The darkness drops again; but now I know
That twenty centuries of stony sleep
Were vexed to nightmare by a rocking cradle,
And what rough beast, its hour come round at last,
Slouches towards Bethlehem to be born?

In the first stanza, we see the image of a falcon and a falconer, symbolically representing the connection between the divine and the earthbound, like Horus from Egyptian mythology. The widening gyre symbolizes chaos and disconnection, with the falcon losing touch with the divine guidance of the falconer in a spiral that expands outward. This imagery reflects the increasing fragmentation of the spiritual world from human experience, resembling the world tree Yggdrasil from Norse mythology, where the branches of knowledge extend farther from the central trunk, becoming less coherent.

The phrase "Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold" invokes an apocalyptic scenario reminiscent of Ragnarok or the biblical apocalypse, where the very foundations of existence are shaken and dismantled. The following lines describe a world in disarray, with anarchy reigning and the pure being drowned by the "blood-dimmed tide," a possible reference to the destructive waters of floods in mythic narratives, such as the biblical flood or the deluge in the Epic of Gilgamesh.

The second stanza introduces the titular concept, "the Second Coming," which traditionally refers to the return of Christ in Christian eschatology. However, Yeats subverts this notion with the appearance of a monstrous figure "somewhere in sands of the desert," described as having "the head of a man" and "a lion body," a depiction reminiscent of several mythological beings, such as the Egyptian sphinx or the Greek Chimera. This creature's "blank and pitiless" gaze recalls the harsh, life-giving sun in many mythologies, embodying both creative and destructive energies.
