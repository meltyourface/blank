To fully respond to this knowledge challenge, we need to first establish a basic understanding of the key concepts: Nihilism, Hermeticism, and Existentialism. With this foundation in place, we can then analyze the potential consequences, syncretic influences, and possible reconciliations or conflicts between these belief systems as they relate to the rejection of inherent meaning and objective morality central to Nihilism.

Nihilism is a philosophical belief system that asserts the absence of inherent meaning, objective morality, or intrinsic value in the universe. Nihilists argue that traditional religious and philosophical claims of cosmic purpose or objective morality are baseless, with life and existence ultimately devoid of transcendent meaning.

Hermeticism is an ancient esoteric tradition rooted in the teachings attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, a figure often associated with the Greek god Hermes and Egyptian god Thoth. Hermeticism is marked by an emphasis on divine wisdom, spiritual alchemy, and the exploration of natural laws governing the universe. Central to Hermetic thought is the principle "as above, so below," which posits a harmonious relationship between the macrocosm (cosmic order) and microcosm (individuals).

Existentialism is a philosophical movement that emerged in the 19th and 20th centuries as a response to widespread feelings of despair, disillusionment, and disconnection from intrinsic meaning or value in life. Existentialists emphasize the importance of individual experience, free choice, and personal responsibility in the face of an inherently meaningless or absurd existence.

Given the context of Nihilism rejecting inherent meaning and objective morality, we can now analyze its potential consequences and syncretic influences on the philosophical foundations of Hermeticism and Existentialism.

Hermeticism's emphasis on divine wisdom and cosmic order stands in stark contrast to the Nihilistic rejection of objective meaning. The Hermetic principle "as above, so below" posits a harmonious relationship between cosmic order and individual life, suggesting intrinsic meaning and purpose within the universe. Nihilism, by rejecting objective morality and inherent meaning, undermines the Hermetic framework and leaves little room for collaboration between these two systems.

However, Nihilism could potentially influence Hermeticism by prompting a reappraisal of the focus on spiritual alchemy and divine wisdom. In a world devoid of inherent meaning, the search for personal transformation and enlightenment offered by spiritual alchemy could become even more significant, perhaps leading some Hermetic practitioners to adopt elements of existential thought in their quest for purpose and meaning.

On the other hand, Existentialism shares some core tenets with Nihilism, particularly the rejection of objective morality and transcendent meaning. While Nihilism can be seen as a more extreme or pessimistic offshoot of Existentialism, the two systems are fundamentally connected by their insistence on an intrinsically meaningless universe. Nihilism can be thought of as a possible endpoint of Existentialist thought, concerning the implications and consequences of living in a universe devoid of inherent meaning.

In this sense, Existentialism can be seen as a potential bridge between Hermeticism and Nihilism, offering a response to the latter's negation of meaning while retaining the individual-centered focus of the former. By emphasizing individual experience, freedom, and personal responsibility, Existentialism allows for the possibility of finding meaning and value in life, albeit through self-created constructs rather than objective truths or cosmic order.

In summary, the rejection of inherent meaning and objective morality within Nihilism presents significant challenges to the philosophical foundations of Hermeticism, while also revealing some potential syncretic influences that may serve to reshape Hermetic thought. Existentialism, as a response to this shared rejection of objective meaning, may serve as a potential bridge between these systems, highlighting reconciliations as well as conflicts in their fundamental tenets. Ultimately, the exploration of these philosophical systems' interactions and influences can deepen our understanding of their individual contributions to the ongoing search for truth, meaning, and understanding in an increasingly complex world.
