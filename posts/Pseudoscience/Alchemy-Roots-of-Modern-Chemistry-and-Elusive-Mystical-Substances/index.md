To evaluate the relationship between the concept of alchemy and the development of modern chemistry, let us begin by understanding what alchemy is and its historical relevance. Alchemy is an ancient philosophical and protoscientific practice dating back to Greco-Roman Egypt, which combined elements of spirituality, chemistry, metallurgy, and physics. Its primary aim was to discover the fundamental secrets of the universe, particularly by transmuting base metals like lead into noble metals like gold. Key aspects of alchemy include the Philosopher's Stone, the Elixir of Life, and other mystical substances and practices, which were believed to hold the secrets to improving human life and gaining greater understanding of the world.

The Philosopher's Stone is a legendary substance in alchemy that is thought to have the power to transform base metals into gold, as well as provide the Elixir of Life - a potion granting eternal youth and immortality. The search for the Philosopher's Stone was more than a quest for material wealth; it represented an aspiration for spiritual and intellectual enlightenment.

The Elixir of Life, as previously mentioned, was believed to confer eternal youth and life. The search for this elixir was a key driver in the practice of alchemy, as it was theorized that it could be created through a series of chemical processes and transformations.

Now that we have an understanding of the key aspects of alchemy, we can explore how it contributed to the development of modern chemistry. While many aspects of alchemy were grounded in myth and pseudoscience, the experimental processes it involved laid the foundation for the scientific method and the field of chemistry. Alchemists invented various tools, equipment, and techniques that are still used in modern chemistry, such as distillation, crystallization, and the use of acids and bases. 

Additionally, alchemy also contributed to early understandings of various elements and compounds. For instance, the alchemists' experimentation with matter led them to classify materials as acids or bases, laying the groundwork for theories of elemental interaction. The classification of elements by alchemists, although often based on symbolic and mystical meanings, would later be refined into the periodic table that we recognize today.

However, it is essential to recognize that the pseudoscientific elements within alchemy, such as the belief in the Philosopher's Stone and Elixir of Life, have complicated its impact on the progress and understanding of legitimate chemistry. As alchemy was often intertwined with esotericism and mysticism, it gave rise to a sense of secrecy and misinformation, which led to the spread of false and misleading ideas.

Despite these challenges, alchemy's influence on modern chemistry cannot be denied. As the forerunner to modern chemistry, alchemy fostered an interest in understanding the natural world and the materials we interact with daily. Many fundamental chemical principles, including the conservation of mass and the process of experimentation, can be traced back to the work of alchemists.

In summary, alchemy played a significant role in the development of modern chemistry by providing early experimental techniques, equipment, and classifications of elements that would later be refined into the systematic discipline of chemistry. While pseudoscientific elements of alchemy, such as the Philosopher's Stone and the Elixir of Life, have complicated its historical legacy, it is crucial to acknowledge the foundational influence alchemy has had on helping humanity pursue understanding and mastery over the world's natural elements.
