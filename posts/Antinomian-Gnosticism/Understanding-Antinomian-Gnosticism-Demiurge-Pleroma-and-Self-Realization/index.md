In this response, we will explore the dialectics between three pivotal concepts in Antinomian Gnosticism: the Demiurge, the Pleroma, and individual self-realization. We will analyze their interconnectedness in the pursuit of spiritual transcendence from the limitations imposed by prevailing moral and social norms. The dialectics within Antinomian Gnosticism offer a profound understanding of the cosmos, the individual soul, and spiritual liberation. By examining how these concepts are interrelated, we will better understand the ultimate goal of spiritual transcendence.

1. The Demiurge

In Gnostic thought, the Demiurge is a creator deity, often depicted as a lower, less divine being than the true God. This entity is responsible for creating and governing the material universe, and is also identified with the ruling god of the Old Testament. The Demiurge's creation is seen as imperfect and flawed, characterized by suffering, ignorance, and illusion. In Antinomian Gnosticism, followers view prevailing moral and social norms as constructs of the Demiurge, which result in limiting the spiritual growth of individuals and keeping them trapped in the material world.

2. The Pleroma

The Pleroma, in contrast, represents the realm of divine fullness, emanations, or aspects of the ultimate Godhead. It is the transcendent reality beyond the visible world created by the Demiurge. The Pleroma contains aeons, divine entities collectively forming the totality of the true, transcendent spiritual reality. Gnostics seek to reunite with the Pleroma and escape the limitations and constraints of the material world and its moral and social norms.

3. Individual Self-Realization

Individual self-realization in Antinomian Gnosticism is a process by which a person awakens and realizes their divine nature. This inner gnosis, or knowledge, allows them to transcend the limitations imposed by the Demiurge's world. Self-realization is achieved by gaining insight into the true nature of the cosmos and one's own divine origin, as well as rejecting the artificial moral and social norms that keep them imprisoned in the material realm.

Interconnectedness of Concepts:

The pursuit of spiritual transcendence in Antinomian Gnosticism involves navigating the dialectics between the Demiurge, the Pleroma, and individual self-realization. Understanding the nature of the Demiurge and the imperfect world it governs enables Gnostics to perceive the inadequacies of prevailing moral and social norms. 

On the other hand, by turning towards the Pleroma and realizing one's divine nature, Gnostics can initiate a process of spiritual ascent, transcending the Demiurge's control and its imposed norms. The process of individual self-realization thus serves as the catalyst to break free from the bondage created by the Demiurge and to enter the realm of divine fullness.

Conclusion:

In Antinomian Gnosticism, the concepts of the Demiurge, Pleroma, and individual self-realization are interconnected in the pursuit of spiritual transcendence from the limitations of prevailing moral and social norms. The Demiurge represents the imperfect creator-god of the material world, and its constructs function to confine the individual. In contrast, the Pleroma symbolizes the realm of divine fullness. Through the process of individual self-realization, a person can cultivate transformative knowledge to transcend the constraints imposed by the Demiurge and reunite with the divine realm of the Pleroma.
