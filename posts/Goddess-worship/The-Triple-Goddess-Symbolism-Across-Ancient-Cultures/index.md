The Triple Goddess is a prominent archetype found in various ancient cultures, symbolizing the essential aspects of the feminine divine. This powerful figure represents the three stages of a woman's life and is commonly depicted as the Maiden, the Mother, and the Crone. In this response, I will delve into the symbolic significance of the Triple Goddess across diverse ancient cultures, drawing correlations between their interpretations in connection with the cycles of nature and the universe. 

To begin, let's examine the Triple Goddess archetype in Ancient Greek mythology. Here, the three goddesses known as the Moirai, or "Fates," are often recognized as the embodiment of this feminine triad. They are Clotho (spinner of life thread/Maiden), Lachesis (measurer of the thread/Mother), and Atropos (cutter of the thread/Crone). In this portrayal, the Maiden symbolizes youth and potential, the Mother represents fertility and life, while the Crone signifies wisdom and death. These stages correspond to the phases of the Moon - waxing crescent (Maiden), full moon (Mother), and waning crescent (Crone), and are also associated with the agricultural cycles of planting, growth, and harvest.

In Celtic mythology, the Triple Goddess is represented by the Morrigan, a powerful war goddess who transcended the boundaries of life and death. While she is often depicted as a single entity, the Morrigan is also interchangeable with a triad of goddesses - Macha, Badb, and Anu. Each goddess displays traits of the Maiden, Mother, and Crone respectively, aligned with the seasonal changes of land fertility, birth, and decay. In this context, the Triple Goddess represents the power of transformation, sovereignty, and conflict, all related to the natural cycles.

In Hindu tradition, the Triple Goddess is manifested through the trinity of deities, known as "Tridevi." The trinity comprises Saraswati (the goddess of knowledge and arts/Maiden), Lakshmi (the goddess of wealth and prosperity/Mother), and Kali or Durga (the goddess of time, change, and destruction/Crone). These personifications represent cosmic creation, preservation, and destruction cycles aligned with the basic principles of existence.
