To fully respond to this knowledge challenge regarding Gnostic cosmology and Aeons, it's essential to begin by breaking the question down into its components: historical interpretations, spiritual implications, metaphorical undertones, and how these aspects affect the understanding of the cosmic order. We will then examine how different Gnostic sects tackle the paradoxes inherent in their beliefs.

Historical Interpretations:
To understand historical interpretations of Gnostic cosmology, it is necessary to appreciate the context within which Gnosticism evolved. Gnosticism was a religious movement prevalent throughout the Mediterranean and the Middle East between the 2nd and 4th centuries AD. While it incorporated aspects of Platonic, Neoplatonic, and Abrahamic cosmologies, Gnosticism developed its unique cosmological views.

The Gnostic cosmic framework typically involves a Supreme Being or Monad emanating lesser beings – Aeons – to facilitate its intention. Aeons are paired, masculine and feminine entities, and reflect aspects of the divine. Gnostic cosmology contends that the reality we see is an illusion, primarily brought forth by a flawed Aeon, called the Demiurge, who created the material world.

Spiritual Implications:

The notion of emanation implies that on an ontological level, everything derives from the divine. This creates a connection between an individual's inner spiritual essence and the divine source. Spiritual salvation, in Gnostic thought, hinges on an individual's understanding of their divine origins and the process of reuniting with the ultimate Godhead. This return often involves transcending the material world and recognizing the illusory nature of reality.

An integral aspect of Gnostic teachings is the concept of gnosis – the direct knowledge of the divine which transcends mere intellectual understanding. The pursuit of gnosis generally involves studying sacred texts, meditating, and participating in initiatory rites taught by knowledgeable masters.

Metaphorical Undertones:

Metaphorically, the process of emanation can serve as an allegory of the creative impulse behind the world. The act of stemming from a singular source, diversifying into various forms, and then reuniting with the origin reflects the Gnostic understanding of the life journey. Emanation and reunification symbolize the cycles of emanation, return and integration. The reintegration relies on esoteric knowledge or gnosis that an individual must strive to acquire.

Reconciling Paradoxes and Understanding the Cosmic Order:

Various historical Gnostic sects, such as the Sethians, Valentinians, and Marcionites, embraced different interpretations of cosmology. However, they all persevered to reconcile the inherent paradoxes in their beliefs: the contrasting concepts of the ultimate divine and flawed creation, the material world as evil yet emanating from a good source, and the mixture of divine sparks within corrupted matter.

The Valentinian notion of a rescue mission to capture divine sparks entrapped in matter addressed and offered a solution to the paradox of the flawed material world. Other sects, like the Sethians, saw the Demiurge as a lower emanation that had gone astray; he was ignorant of his divine origins and thus could create flawed demiurgic creations.

In conclusion, Gnostic cosmology and the Aeons concept are a complex synthesis of historical context, spiritual implications, and metaphorical undertones. The historical interpretations and embrace of emanation inform the spiritual practices aimed at attaining gnosis. Meanwhile, metaphorical narratives of emanation can be read as allegory for the initiation journey. Through the understanding of these aspects, initiates learn to make sense of the cosmic order and transcend the material world that binds them.
