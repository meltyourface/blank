Gothic Revival architecture emerged in the 18th century as a reaction against the pervasive rationalism and neoclassicism that characterized the Enlightenment era. As an aesthetic movement, it drew inspiration from medieval Gothic architecture, emphasizing verticality, intricate ornamentation, and emotionally evocative forms. It was deeply connected to the Romantic movement, which valued intuition, personal expression, and a sense of wonder, often embracing supernatural and spiritual elements.

Two key figures in the Gothic Revival movement were Horace Walpole and Augustus Pugin. Walpole, an English writer and politician, is considered one of the first proponents of Gothic Revival architecture. Strawberry Hill, his country villa, was an early and influential example of the revivalist style, incorporating features like pointed arches, asymmetrical planning, and intricate ornamentation. Walpole saw Gothic architecture as an expression of national identity and a link to a romanticized English past.

Augustus Pugin, an architect and designer, played a crucial role in the development of Gothic Revival architecture in the 19th century. His work was deeply influenced by his conversion to Catholicism, and he saw Gothic architecture as inherently spiritual and morally superior to classical styles. Pugin's designs for the Palace of Westminster and the interior of the House of Lords exemplify his commitment to the Gothic style, which he believed was the true architectural expression of Christian ideals.

There is an intricate relationship between Gothic Revival architecture and esoteric symbolism. The intricate ornamentation and designs often incorporated into this architecture allude to spiritual principles, alchemical processes, and mystic elements found in occult traditions. In addition, the emphasis on verticality and soaring height in Gothic design implied a longing for transcendence, a connection to the divine, and an aspiration towards spiritual awakening. These architectural choices differed significantly from the traditional Enlightenment values, which tended to prioritize reason, empiricism, and secularism over spiritual concerns.

Reflecting upon the connections between Gothic Revival architecture, mysticism, and spiritualism, we can contrast this movement against key Enlightenment ideals. Where the Enlightenment sought to elevate rationalism, Gothic Revival architecture expressed the emotions, spirituality, and nostalgia for a seemingly purer past. Moreover, the Enlightenment's emphasis on measurable progress and worldly achievements contrasted with the Gothic Revival's focus on the eternal, transcendent, and otherworldly realities.

Within the broader context of the occult and the pursuit of transcendental knowledge, Gothic Revival architecture serves as a symbolic representation of humankind's longing for understanding of the mysteries of life, as well as our desire to connect with the divine. The movement's revival of medieval forms and incorporation of esoteric symbolism illustrate its deep ties to the quest for spiritual enlightenment and a return to a lost, sacred past.

In conclusion, the 18th-century Gothic Revival architecture movement, spearheaded by significant designers such as Horace Walpole and Augustus Pugin, embodied mysticism, spiritualism, and a deep longing for the past. This architectural style incorporated esoteric symbolism, contrasting with Enlightenment-era values. The implications of this connection can be observed in the broader context of the occult and the human pursuit of transcendental knowledge, as Gothic Revival architecture represented the yearning for an understanding of the mysterious, divine, and transcendent aspects of existence.
