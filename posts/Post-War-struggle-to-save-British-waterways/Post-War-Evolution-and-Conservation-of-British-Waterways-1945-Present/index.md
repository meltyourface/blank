  Chronology of the Post-War Struggle to Save British Waterways (1945-Present):
  
  1945:
  - World War II ends, leaving Britain with enormous war debts and a drastic need to rebuild the nation's infrastructure.
  
  1948:
  - Nationalization of British Waterways under the British Transport Commission (BTC), with the responsibility of managing and maintaining over 2,000 miles of waterways.
  
  1950s:
  - Industrial decline leads to reduced use of canals for commercial transportation. The focus shifts towards leisure use, with the first rally organized on the River Thames in 1950.
  - Tom Rolt's book "Narrow Boat" published in 1944, spurs interest in restoring the waterways for leisure purposes.
  
  1951:
  - Ineos Hutchinson (later Sir Ineos) becomes the first chair of the Inland Waterways Restoration Committee.
  
  1954:
  - The British Waterways Board (BWB) is established as a result of the Transport Act, providing a more focused approach to managing and restoring the waterways.
  
  1955:
  - The Manchester Ship Canal is closed to commercial traffic, highlighting the decline of industrial use of waterways.
  
  1962:
  - The Transport Act allows the BWB to prioritize leisure activities as a key aspect of waterways management alongside commercial activities.
  
  1963:
  - The Langmire report is published, assessing the future of waterways and recommending investments in distinct waterways. This includes London's canals, the Kennet and Avon Canal, and the River Wey.
  
  1964:
  - The Inland Waterways Association (IWA) founded by Robert Aickman and L.T.C. Rolt, which campaigns vigorously for protection and preservation of Britain's canals and navigable rivers.
  
  1972:
  - British Waterways Authorities (Scotland) Bill receives Royal Assent, ensuring that Scottish waterways will remain under public control.
  
  1983:
  - The National Waterways Museum (formerly British Waterways Museum) opens in Gloucester, showcasing the history of Britain's waterways.
  
  1992:
  - The Wigan Pier Quarter is officially opened by H.R.H. the Prince of Wales, marking a commitment to waterfront regeneration.
  
  2004:
  - The British Waterways Advisory Forum is established, providing input and advice on various waterway issues.
  
  2010:
  - British Waterways transitions to a charity, becoming the Canal & River Trust in England and Wales, and Scottish Canals in Scotland.
  
  2012:
  - The Canal & River Trust and Scottish Canals are officially launched, giving the responsibility of managing the waterways to these new organizations.
  
  2017:
  - The UK Government announces a Â£1.6 million fund for the restoration of waterways, particularly focusing on the Cotswold Canals, and the Melton Mowbray Navigation and Oakham Canal.
  
  2020:
  - The Environment Bill is introduced, with provisions aimed at improving water quality and increasing biodiversity in British waterways.
  
  Throughout the chronology, key figures include Tom Rolt, Ineos Hutchinson, Robert Aickman, and LTC Rolt, who helped shape the direction of British waterways policies and public engagement. Key milestones include the establishment of the Inland Waterways Association (IWA), the establishment of the Canal & River Trust and Scottish Canals, along with pivotal policies and investments made by the UK Government.
