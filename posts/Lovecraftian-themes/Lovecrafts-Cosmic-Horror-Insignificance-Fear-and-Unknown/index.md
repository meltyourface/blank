To begin with, it is essential to understand the core ideas underpinning Lovecraft's literary themes and the rationale behind each concept. Cosmicism, the fear of the unknown, and humanity's insignificance are central elements that run through the vast majority of Lovecraft's works, including "The Call of Cthulhu," "At the Mountains of Madness," and "The Shadow over Innsmouth." They are part and parcel of a wider Lovecraftian horror framework that revolves around humans being powerless and insignificant in the face of an indifferent, immense, and unknowable universe.

Starting with the concept of cosmicism, it refers to the notion that human beings are not at the center of the universe; instead, they are inconsequential in the grand cosmic scale. This philosophical view negates the anthropocentric beliefs of humanity's importance, possessiveness, or uniqueness in the universe. In Lovecraft's works, cosmicism manifests through his complex pantheon of godlike cosmic entities and the revelation that human beings are mere puppets in a vast cosmic play.

The fear of the unknown is closely linked with cosmicism. Much of the horror in Lovecraft's works derives from the confrontation with the unknown â€“ fears that humans cannot comprehend, forces that exist beyond their understanding, and the realization that knowledge can be terrifying. This fear arises from cognitive dissonance and the inability to reconcile their beliefs with the reality they encounter. In Lovecraft's fiction, individuals are often thrust into situations they cannot fathom, which erodes their sanity and self-concept.

The insignificance of humanity is the direct outcome of the two previously mentioned ideas. Humans, according to Lovecraft, are insignificant not only in their cosmic standing but also in their capacity for rationality and their struggle to cope with the fear of the unknown. The protagonists in Lovecraft's stories often struggle to maintain their sanity when faced with unimaginable horrors, a testament to the fragility and limitations of human comprehension.

These concepts interrelate extraordinarily as they contribute to the bleakness and terror pervading Lovecraft's fictional universe. In "The Call of Cthulhu," for instance, the protagonists come face-to-face with the ancient, hibernating cosmic entity named Cthulhu. The mere sight or thought of it drives people to insanity, as Cthulhu represents an unfathomable power that challenges the very foundation of human rationality.

Similarly, in "At the Mountains of Madness," a group of explorers comes across an ancient, pre-human civilization buried beneath the Antarctic ice for eons. The entity responsible for the downfall of this ancient civilization turns out to be vastly superior and indifferent to human existence. The revelation shatters the explorers' illusion of humanity's perceived greatness and upends their understanding of history and the world.

Lastly, "The Shadow over Innsmouth" features a remote, degenerate human society that had entered into a pact with the inhuman, aquatic Deep Ones. In exchange for prosperity and prolonged life, the people of Innsmouth have to interbreed with these monstrous beings and ultimately lose their humanity. This narrative starkly highlights the relative insignificance of human values in the grander cosmic scheme.

In conclusion, the intersection of cosmicism, the fear of the unknown, and the insignificance of humanity within Lovecraft's works, as exemplified by "The Call of Cthulhu," "At the Mountains of Madness," and "The Shadow over Innsmouth," creates a consistent and overarching theme that is deeply rooted in Lovecraftian horror. These concepts reveal the vulnerability of human existence, underscore the limits of human knowledge and rationality, and evoke a sense of existential terror. By presenting narratives where protagonists confront these realities, Lovecraft aims to inspire not only visceral fear but also a profound sense of awe and humility as readers face the vast, indifferent, and enigmatic cosmos surrounding them.
