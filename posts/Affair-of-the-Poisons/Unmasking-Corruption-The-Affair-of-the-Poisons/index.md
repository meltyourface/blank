In this era, the lines between religious, political, and occult spheres were blurred, allowing for close connections between various factions. Religion, particularly Catholicism, had a profound influence on politics and society, with the Church holding an inordinate amount of authority and control. Simultaneously, interest in the occult, alchemy, divination, and sorcery was on the rise.
  
  2. **Key Players and Events**:
  
  The Affair of the Poisons began with the trial and execution of Madame de Brinvilliers, a French aristocrat who was accused of poisoning her father and brothers in order to inherit the family fortune. Her trial led to further investigations into suspected poisoners, arcanists, and fortune-tellers, revealing the staggering scope of the scandal. Central figures included Madame de Montespan (the king's mistress), Catherine Monvoisin (also known as La Voisin, a prominent fortune-teller), and various individuals of the aristocracy who were accused of poisonous intrigue and dabbling in the occult. The extent of their criminal enterprises included poisoning rivals, conducting black masses, and even attempts to poison the king himself.

  The Affair of the Poisons exposed the intricate web of religious, political, and occult power structures that had permeated across French society. A key example is the role of the Catholic Church, which found itself implicated in numerous scandals and allegations. Priests were accused of conducting black masses for aristocrats and witches, providing spiritual backing to occult activities and undermining the moral authority of the Church. This revelation exposed the hypocrisy prevalent within the religious sphere and revealed its intersections with the world of the occult.

The connection between the king's mistress, Madame de Montespan, and La Voisin placed the institution of the monarchy in an unflattering light. The fact that many other aristocrats were also implicated in these occult activities further revealed the depths of moral decay in the highest echelons of French society.
  
  These intersecting religious, political, and occult connections were exacerbated by societal elements such as fascination with the mysteries of the occult, a desire for power, and the use of dark rituals to achieve personal gains.

  The Affair of the Poisons challenged the societal norms and moral compass of 17th-century France in various ways.
