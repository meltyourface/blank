1. The Oracle of Delphi (Ancient Greece)
The Oracle of Delphi, also known as Pythia, was a highly revered priestess in Ancient Greece who would convey messages from the god Apollo to seekers of divine wisdom. The practice of seeking prophetic guidance through the Oracle reflected the Ancient Greeks' belief in the divine and their reliance on divine communication to make decisions and shape their destiny. The intricacies of the Pythian rituals, often involving supernatural forces, and the Oracle's cryptic responses, which were open to interpretation, served as symbolic representations of the mysterious and divine nature of prophetic communication.

2. The Revelation of St. John the Divine (Christianity)
The Book of Revelation is the final book of the New Testament in the Christian Bible, often attributed to St. John the Divine. It records visions and prophetic messages received by John, which he believed were sent by God and heralded the end of the world. The highly symbolic and apocalyptic nature of the revelation illustrates the profound transformative power of divine communication, as it has impacted the interpretation of Christianity, as well as the religious art and culture throughout the centuries.

Guru Nanak, the first of the ten Sikh Gurus, is known for sharing divine wisdom received through conversations with God. These experiences gave rise to the sacred text, Guru Granth Sahib, one of the primary sources of Sikh spiritual knowledge. The significance of Guru Nanak's divine encounters within Sikhism emphasizes the importance of prophetic communication in the culturation and preservation of religious values and teachings.
