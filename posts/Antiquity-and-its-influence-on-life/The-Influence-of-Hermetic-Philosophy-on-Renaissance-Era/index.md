To answer this knowledge challenge, we must first understand the Hermetic principles, their roots in ancient Egyptian and Greek occultism, and the connections and influences these principles had on European Renaissance society. We will then evaluate the way in which these philosophical and esoteric notions shaped life during the Renaissance period, focusing on their impact on philosophy, science, and culture. Lastly, we will explore how these influences can be traced in the primary esoteric works and figures of both eras.

The Hermetic principles originated in the writings attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, a legendary figure who was considered the embodiment of the ancient Egyptian god Thoth and the Greek god Hermes. These principles encompass a range of philosophical, mystical, and esoteric ideas that center around the interconnectedness of all things. They are encapsulated within the Emerald Tablet, an ancient Hermetic text that contains the famous phrase "As above, so below," which alludes to the correspondence between the macrocosm (the universe as a whole) and the microcosm (the individual human being). Other key Hermetic themes include the concepts of mentalism (the idea that everything is created by thought), vibration (the idea that everything is in constant motion), and polarity (the idea that opposites are two aspects of the same thing).

Now, let us examine the ways in which Hermetic principles shaped critical aspects of life during the European Renaissance. The Renaissance was a period of intellectual and artistic rebirth that began in the 14th century in Italy and spread across Europe. This flourishing of learning and culture was spurred by the rediscovery of many classical and esoteric texts that had been lost to the Western world for centuries.

1. **Philosophy**: The Hermetic principles had a considerable impact on Renaissance philosophy, particularly in the realm of Neoplatonism, an ancient philosophical system revived during the Renaissance. Many Neoplatonic philosophers, such as Marsilio Ficino and Pico della Mirandola, were deeply influenced by Hermeticism and sought to reconcile its teachings with Christian theology. They believed that the universe was infused with divine wisdom and that attaining mystical knowledge could lead to union with the divine.

2. **Science**: Hermetic principles also played a pivotal role in shaping the direction of scientific inquiry during the European Renaissance. Alchemy, a discipline deeply rooted in Hermetic thought, aimed to explore the interconnectedness of all things and sought to transform base metals into gold. While its goals were often met with skepticism, alchemy laid the groundwork for future developments in chemistry. Similarly, Hermetic principles played a significant role in shaping the field of astronomy, as figures like Johannes Kepler and Tycho Brahe sought to understand the celestial bodies and their influence on human life within the context of a Hermetically-informed cosmos.

3. **Culture**: The influence of the Hermetic principles can be seen in various aspects of Renaissance culture. Artworks from the period often incorporated symbolic elements that echoed Hermetic teachings, such as the Vitruvian Man by Leonardo da Vinci, which illustrated the correspondence between the proportions of the human body and the geometry of the cosmos. Enigmatic works like the Hypnerotomachia Poliphili, an allegorical tale laden with esoteric symbolism, incorporated Hermetic concepts into a complex narrative.

To trace the influences of Hermetic principles on the primary esoteric works and figures of both eras, one can look at seminal texts like the Corpus Hermeticum, which was rediscovered and translated during the Renaissance, sparking a renewed interest in Hermeticism. Key figures including Renaissance philosophers like Ficino and Mirandola, as well as alchemists such as Paracelsus and Isaac Newton, are evidence of the profound influence of Hermetic principles on the intellectual landscape of the time.

In conclusion, the Hermetic principles of ancient Egyptian and Greek occultism profoundly shaped the critical aspects of life in European Renaissance society. These principles influenced the realms of philosophy, science, and culture, fostering a climate of intellectual exploration and creativity. The connections between the two eras can be traced in primary esoteric works and influential figures, testifying to the enduring legacy of Hermeticism in shaping the development of Western thought.
