To address this knowledge challenge, there are three primary concepts to tackle: the alchemical stage of Nigredo in the Magnum Opus, Aleister Crowley's concept of crossing the Abyss, and Carl Jung's psychological concept of the Shadow within his individuation process. Let us begin by looking at each concept individually before drawing parallels and discussing the integration of these insights in the process of understanding the Self in relation to the microcosm and macrocosm.

1. Nigredo in the Magnum Opus:
Nigredo, the blackening or melanosis, represents the first stage of the alchemical magnum opus or the great work of transformation. This phase involves the decomposition and purification of matter, which is understood as the breaking down and dissolution of the ego and the negative aspects of the personality. It is a time of darkness, difficulty, and introspection that ultimately serves as the foundation for the transformation and renewal of the individual or substance undergoing the alchemical process.

2. Aleister Crowley's crossing the Abyss:
The concept of crossing the Abyss is a key component in Aleister Crowley's system of Thelema. The Abyss represents the gulf that separates the phenomenal world (microcosm) from the divine reality (macrocosm). In the spiritual journey of the aspirant, crossing the Abyss represents the shedding of the illusory self and the integration of the True Will. This transformative process often involves confronting and transcending one's fears, desires, and limitations. Successfully crossing the Abyss symbolizes the achievement of spiritual liberation and reactivation of divine consciousness.

3. Carl Jung's concept of the Shadow:
In Jungian psychology, the Shadow is an essential aspect of the individuation process. The Shadow represents the unconscious, darker, and often rejected parts of our psyches that we may not be aware of. Through the process of individuation, one is encouraged to confront, acknowledge, and integrate these hidden aspects of their personality in order to achieve psychological wholeness and self-realization.

Now that we have a basic understanding of each concept, we can draw parallels between them:
- All three concepts emphasize the importance of confronting, understanding, and integrating darker aspects of one's self in the process of spiritual or psychological transformation.
- Each concept signifies a process of dismantling the ego or false self in order to reveal the true Self and establish a connection with the divine or universal consciousness.
- These processes involve a symbolic death and rebirth, emphasizing the cyclical nature of transformation and understanding that self-development is a continuous, iterative effort.

To achieve a transformative understanding of the Self in relation to the microcosm and macrocosm, a seeker of Truth should integrate their findings from these three concepts as follows:

1. Acknowledge and confront the darker aspects of their personality (Shadow) and understand the significance of integrating these qualities into their conscious awareness.
2. Use the alchemical process of Nigredo as a metaphor for their path, embracing the understanding that the spiritual journey often involves pain, difficulty, and self-sacrifice.
3. Strive to cross the Abyss, by assessing and transcending their limitations, fears, and desires in order to align with their True Will and establish a harmony between the microcosm and macrocosm.

In conclusion, the parallels between Nigredo in the Magnum Opus, Aleister Crowley's concept of crossing the Abyss, and Carl Jung's psychological concept of the Shadow all emphasize the importance of self-reflection, spiritual and psychological transformation, and integration of the darker aspects of our beings. By understanding these concepts and their relationships to each other, a seeker of Truth can achieve greater self-realization and maintain a deeper connection to both the microcosmic and macrocosmic aspects of existence.
