The Philosopher's Stone was a legendary substance, based on centuries-old alchemical and mystical theories, that was considered to have the power to catalyze the base metals' transformation into gold, as well as the power to grant immortality in the form of the Elixir of Life.

At its core, alchemy was concerned with exploring the nature of the physical world, cosmic principles, the role of human beings, and the harmony between the cosmos and the microcosm. It was a primarily experimental and theoretical pursuit that embodied both practical and spiritual aspects. Some of the key goals of alchemy were the production of panaceas, development of plant and mineral-based medicines, and the understanding of the laws governing the transformation of matter.

Option 1 primarily deals with this pursuit of transformation and understanding. One of the key principles in alchemy is that all physical matter consists of four primal elements: earth, air, fire, and water. These elements are associated with qualities such as hot, cold, wet, and dry. In alchemical practice, the manipulation of these elements and the balance between their qualities is essential to achieve the desired effects.

The concept of transmuting primal elements through a multi-stage process is fundamentally in line with the established alchemical theories. The Magnum Opus (Great Work) describes the stages an alchemist must go through to create the Philosopher's Stone, involving processes such as calcination, dissolution, separation, conjunction, fermentation, distillation, and coagulation. These stages require a combination of chemical techniques and philosophical knowledge.
