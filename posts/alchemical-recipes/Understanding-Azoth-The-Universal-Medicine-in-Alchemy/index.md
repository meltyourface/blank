**Explanation**:
The philosopher's stone, a powerful symbol in alchemy, represents the ultimate quest of transforming base metals into nobler elements, such as gold, as well as the attainment of immortality. Throughout the history of alchemy, there are several key elements and processes associated with the creation of the philosopher's stone.

In the context of this question, Azoth is not utilized in a traditional alchemical recipe for the philosopher's stone, although it does play an essential role in the alchemical tradition.

Azoth, a term derived from the Arabic word "al-zā'būq," meaning mercury, is often considered the "universal medicine" or "universal solvent" in the alchemical tradition. Although sometimes equated to Mercury (as per the Arabic meaning), it is distinct from the actual element Mercury utilized in the alchemical recipe for the philosopher's stone. It is associated with the process of fermentation, a stage within the alchemical Magnum Opus (Great Work).

The other elements mentioned in the question are part of the traditional alchemical recipe for the philosopher's stone:

1. **Mercury - Nigredo**: Mercury, also known as the "prima materia" or "first matter," is a critical element in the alchemical process. Nigredo, the first stage of creating the philosopher's stone, involves breaking down or "putrefying" the substance through calcination and dissolution. In this stage, often associated with the color black, the alchemist seeks to strip away impurities and return the matter to its initial state.

2. **Sulfur - Albedo**: After the purification of Nigredo, alchemists focused on the process of Albedo. This stage involves washing, often symbolized by the color white, and is governed by the element Sulfur. During this phase, the alchemist seeks to purify their intents further and achieve a perfect balance between Mercury and Sulfur, representing a reconciling of opposites.

3. **Salt - Rubedo**: The final stage, Rubedo, involves coagulation and integration, associated with the reddening or becoming whole of the substance. The alchemist adds the element Salt, representing wisdom and divine knowledge, to the mixture. In this stage, the philosopher's stone has been completed, symbolizing the attainment of spiritual perfection or enlightenment.

In conclusion, although Azoth plays an essential role within the alchemical tradition, it is not utilized in a traditional alchemical recipe for the philosopher's stone. Instead, Mercury, Sulfur, and Salt are critical elements in the three main stages, Nigredo, Albedo, and Rubedo, leading to the creation of the philosopher's stone.
