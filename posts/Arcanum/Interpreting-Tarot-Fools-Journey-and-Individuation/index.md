To respond to the knowledge challenge, let us first establish our understanding of the key concepts involved. The major arcana within Tarot refers to a deck of 22 cards, each depicting distinct archetypes representing various spiritual, psychological, and material aspects of worldly experience. The Fool's Journey is a metaphor describing the path that an individual undertakes throughout their life, as depicted through the traversal of the major arcana cards. The hermetic axiom "As above, so below" is a fundamental principle in the study of the occult, broadly suggesting a holistic interconnectedness between the universe's macrocosm and microcosm. Finally, individuation, a Jungian concept, refers to the process of achieving self-realization and integration by reconciling the opposing aspects of one's own psyche into a harmonious whole.

With the context established, we can begin to analyze the symbolic transformation and underlying correlation between the Fool's Journey, the hermetic axiom, and the process of individuation within Arcanum.

The Fool's Journey, as a metaphor for spiritual growth and personal development, begins with the Fool card (numbered 0), representing the individual's state of naivety, potential, and innate curiosity. As the individual progresses through the major arcana cards, they encounter and assimilate the critical lessons of each archetype. This series of encounters subsequently facilitates their transformation into a more actualized and spiritually awakened self. In essence, the Fool's Journey illustrates the concept of individuation.

The hermetic axiom "As above, so below" can be interpreted as both an external reflection and internal integration of the spiritual lessons present in the Fool's Journey. The Tarot's major arcana operate as symbolic representations of the intellectual and spiritual concepts that govern our internal (microcosm) and external (macrocosm) worlds. This principle underlines the interconnectedness between the individual's personal journey into self-realization and the universal laws they discover along the way.

Integrating the Tree of Life (Kabbalah) and archetypal symbolism (Jungian psychology) serve to further illuminate the interwoven nature of these concepts. In Kabbalistic philosophy, the Tree of Life represents the manifold aspects of divine wisdom and knowledge cascading through ten sephiroth (emanations from the ineffable divine). Each sephira corresponds with various aspects of human consciousness and the divine principles that govern the universe. Similarly, the major arcana can be mapped onto the Tree of Life, highlighting the intricate relationships and overarching principles guiding both the personal individuation process and the universal order.

Jungian psychology, in its focus on archetypal symbolism, lends further depth to the study of the Tarot and individuation. The archetypes represented in the major arcana can be understood as constructs of the collective unconscious, representing universally shared patterns, symbols, and myths. In pursuing individuation, an individual confronts and integrates these archetypal aspects within their psyche, arriving at a comprehensive knowledge and understanding of their unique self.

In summary, the symbolic transformation and correlation between the Fool's Journey and the hermetic axiom "As above, so below" provide a richly textured narrative that interweaves personal development, esoteric knowledge, and collective experience. The Fool's Journey represents the individual's quest for individuation and self-realization within the broader context of universal interconnectedness suggested by the axiom. Integration of the Tree of Life (Kabbalah) and archetypal symbolism (Jungian psychology) adds further layers of complexity, highlighting the profound unity of the human experience embedded in the Tarot's major arcana.
