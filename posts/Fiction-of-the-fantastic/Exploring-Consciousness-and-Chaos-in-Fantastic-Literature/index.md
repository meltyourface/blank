To respond to the knowledge challenge, let us first examine the thematic elements from each literary figure's work: 

1. H.P. Lovecraft's cosmic horror: Lovecraft's works, such as "At the Mountains of Madness" and "The Call of Cthulhu," revolve around humanity's insignificance in the face of unimaginable cosmic horrors and ancient beings. These thematic elements often involve the incapability of human understanding to fully comprehend the reality of the cosmos.

2. Jorge Luis Borges' labyrinthine allegories: Borges, through his short stories like "The Garden of Forking Paths" and "The Library of Babel," explores the endless complexities and layers of meaning and interpretation within human thought, language, and existence. His works often involve labyrinthine constructs, both physical and metaphysical, representing the complex nature of human consciousness and the struggle to find meaning within it.

3. J.R.R. Tolkien's high fantasy: Tolkien's "The Lord of the Rings" series and other works incorporate themes of good versus evil, heroism, and sacrifice. These high fantasy stories are deliberately crafted modern myths, with intricate subcreation and linguistic inventiveness. They represent the eternal struggle between order and chaos, with the balance of power constantly shifting between various mythological archetypes.

Now, let's analyze the interplay between human consciousness, mythological archetypes, and the eternal struggle between order and chaos in these thematic elements:

In Lovecraft's cosmic horror, we see a stark contrast between human consciousness—limited by its scope and understanding—and the cosmic beings that exist beyond our comprehension. These god-like entities represent chaotic forces that shatter the illusion of an ordered, meaningful universe. By tapping into the collective subconscious and our primal fears, Lovecraft forces us to question the limits of human understanding.

Borges' labyrinthine allegories bring human consciousness to the forefront, exploring how language and understanding can bend reality into unfathomable complexities. Borges uses mythological archetypes as symbols of deeper, shared experiences that transcend cultural and personal boundaries. This skillful interplay between order and chaos in his allegorical labyrinths forces the reader to confront the inevitability of uncertainty and the multitude of possible realities.

Tolkien's high fantasy establishes a mythopoetic world, where human consciousness encounters embodiments of absolute good and evil. His intricate, detailed subcreation showcases the eternal struggle between order and chaos, as represented by the various races and power dynamics within Middle-earth. Tolkien's characters exemplify archetypal heroes who face overwhelming destructive forces, highlighting the importance of hope, choice, and personal sacrifice in maintaining balance in a complex reality.

In conclusion, the three thematic elements—Lovecraft's cosmic horror, Borges' labyrinthine allegories, and Tolkien's high fantasy—demonstrate the complex interplay between human consciousness, mythological archetypes, and the eternal struggle between order and chaos in the Fiction of the fantastic. As a result, these narratives provide a deeper understanding of the metaphysical aspects and complex realities that challenge our perceptions, assumptions, and the way we grapple with the unknown.
